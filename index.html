🏰 Martin Lindner — East Prussia / Gumbinnen Origin Statement

Vast numbers of Scots migrated to East Prussia. Prussia had freedom of religion, and the Prussian army needed qualified personnel. Gumbinnen, the region my father's mother came from, was clearly a hotspot. High-ranking British and Scottish noble families such as Stuart, Hamilton, Douglas, and Hay are documented there. This district is located directly next to Königsberg. The area is predominantly rural and heavily wooded, with enormous estates owned by prominent Prussian noble families or belonging to the Hohenzollerns themselves.

Members of the Prussian royal family evidently spent their leisure time there and left their genetic mark over the centuries. Some of their illegitimate descendants were recognized and married into the local nobility, while the majority were placed with bourgeois families and later often became royal estate tenants, estate managers, or foresters. Becoming an estate tenant was extremely expensive — these sinecures always came with Prussian official titles and resulting rights (jurisdiction, police powers). In Prussia such individuals were called "Amtsadel" (official nobility), an elite social class generally related to the local nobility by blood or marriage.

This offered an interesting possibility of being related not only through Scottish immigrants to the kings of England, Scotland, and Ireland, but also through the Hanover-Prussian line of the Hohenzollern dynasty itself.

There also appear to be royal connections through my mother's father's lines, leading into the Dutch and Danish nobility. My kinship to families like von Maydell, von Noval-Zastrow, and de Witt triangulates through cousins from the Dutch patrician family Schouten of Amsterdam, who themselves apparently have Stuart ancestors.

London and Amsterdam are quite close. John of Gaunt lived in neighbouring Belgium. Many crowned heads from all eras spent time in Amsterdam, including Queen Elizabeth Stuart (the Winter Queen) and Christian IV of Denmark and Norway. My parents are MRCA 4th cousins.

What is a cM?
A centimorgan (cM) is a unit of genetic distance. In genealogy, 1 cM roughly equals a 1% chance of recombination per generation. Larger shared segments (higher cM) indicate closer relationships. In this project all segments are 3–7cM — typical of 5th–8th cousin connections (about 150–350 years ago). The 208 SNP threshold used on GEDmatch filters out false positives.
What is phasing?
Phasing means testing a parent (or close relative) alongside you to determine which segments you inherited from which side. If a segment appears in both you AND your mother's kit, it's on your maternal chromosome. "Mom-at" (maternal, confirmed via phasing through a grandparent) is the most reliable — it places the segment on a specific ancestral line. "Unresolved" means no phasing kit covers that pair yet.
What is a triangulation group?
When 3 or more members share overlapping DNA on the same chromosome window, they likely inherited that segment from a single common ancestor. These groups are shown with orange brackets in the chromosome view. The strongest group is Chr 15 (27–30 Mb) — 5 members and both reference kits all overlap there, suggesting a very specific common ancestor at that location.
Why are segments so small (3–7cM)?
Ancestral segments break down through recombination each generation. A 7cM segment shared between two people suggests they are roughly 5th–7th cousins, meaning the common ancestor lived about 150–250 years ago. This project is reaching back 600–800 years to Lusignan ancestors — we're detecting faint but real signals of ancient shared DNA at the edge of what autosomal DNA can reliably detect. The 208-SNP threshold (not the default) filters out most false positives.
What is a haplogroup?
A haplogroup is a group of people sharing a common ancestor on a single line — Y-DNA for paternal (all-male) lines, mtDNA for maternal (all-female) lines. Mary's father and brother carry R-FT166311, an extremely rare haplogroup (<0.01% globally) confirmed in the remains of Tsar Nicholas II. This is a direct paternal line signature — meaning Mary's paternal great-great-grandfather descends in unbroken male line from the Romanov/Holstein-Gottorp dynasty.
What is Eurogenes K13?
A population genetics calculator that breaks your autosomal DNA into 13 ancestral population components (North_Atlantic, Baltic, West_Med, East_Med, West_Asian, etc.). In this project it's used to identify broad population affinities per chromosome — useful for seeing if a shared segment falls in a "Northern European" vs "Southern European" context. It does NOT prove specific ancestry but adds supporting context. The ethnicity bands in the Chromosome tab show the whole-chromosome average from K13.

What does * mean next to a member name?
